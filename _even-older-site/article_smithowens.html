BBR Advisor's Board Article

The Literacy Beginnings of Africans before America
Dr. Mary Ann Smith Owens

Africans were noted for their astronomy, navigation and architectural skills in 1971 by Basil Davidson who wrote that Portuguese explorer Vasco da Gama sailed around the Cape of Good Hope in 1408 and found “tall stone towns of comfort and wealth along the East African coast” (17).  The Africans who lived there were as knowledgeable about charts and compasses as Vasco de Gama and his sailors. Davidson also reported that at the same time Timbuktu, a Moorish city also in East Africa, was noted for the sales of its most profitable commodity, books.

According to Jackson (1970), the Golden Age of West Africa preceded the slave trade and included the history of the great Trading Empires of Ghana (700-1200), Mali (1200-1500), Songhay (1350-1600).  The Ghanaian Empire was founded by the Soninkes people whose clans specialized in government, metalworking (iron, gold, silver), agriculture, fishing, animal husbandry, and the manufacture of clothing.  The Ghanaian Empire’s inexhaustible supply of gold attracted the Malians. In 1230 upon his ascension to the throne, King Sundiata expanded Mali to include what once was Ghana.  Mansa Musa, another great ruler of Mali made a pilgrimage to Mecca in 1324 with an entourage of 60,000. This entourage was financed with 80 camels each carrying 300 pounds of gold dust.  With a highly efficient commissary department and excellent cooks, elaborate meals were prepared for the people at each place they stopped. So much gold was lavished upon his Egyptian hosts that the normalcy of the gold market of Cairo was disrupted for twelve years.

Jackson continued that elsewhere Sunni Ali the Great came to the throne of Songhay in 1464.  A notable accomplishment of his reign was the honorable capitulation of the city of Jenne after a siege that lasted over seven years. During the capitulation no harm came to any of its inhabitants but rather a seven-day feast was held during which Sunni Ali the Great married the widow of the town ruler who had died during the siege.  Jackson described Jenne in this manner.     

Jenne was a valuable prize to the Songhay, since it was a prosperous trading center, containing buildings of attractive design, and surrounded by great scenic beauty.  It was also the home of advanced culture, being the seat of a noted university, which employed a staff of thousands of teachers who gave courses of lectures and conducted researches on a variety of subjects.  Their medical school trained physicians and surgeons of great skill.  Among the difficult surgical operations performed successfully by doctors in Jenne was the removal of cataracts from the human eye.  (215)

Sunni Ali the Great also engulfed much of the old Mali Empire into Songhay.  Before his death in 1492 he had become one of the most famous rulers of his day. 

Clarke (1970) in the introduction to Jackson (1970) reported that Africans were great storytellers.  West Africa, the ancestral home of most African Americans, had a colorful and rich history as well as art and folklore.  In describing the era before the enslavement Clarke stated:

Contrary to a misconception which still prevails, the Africans were familiar with literature and art for many years before their contact with the Western world.  Before the breaking up of the social structure of the West African states of Ghana, Mali, and Songhay, and the internal strife and chaos that made the slave trade possible, the forefathers of the Africans who eventually became slaves in the United States lived in a society where university life was fairly common and scholars were beheld with reverence. (20)

In describing slaves in ancient Egypt, Jackson (1970) quoted Reade’s Martyrdom of Man, “The penalty for the murder of a slave was death; this law exists without parallel in the dark slavery annuls both of ancient and modern times” (105). Jones (1993) summarized conduct towards slaves among Africans of later eras as follows:

Slavery, of course, was nothing new to the Africans.  Like much of Europe in the Middle Ages, many African societies had a feudal structure, in which kings were served by vassals, or nobles, who in turn were served by the mass of the kingdom’s subjects.  In this system, people owed service to those above them in the social hierarchy.  The lowest-ranked Africans could be compared to European surfs, who as near slaves had no choice but to work for their masters.  Certain criminals, outcasts and prisoners of war enjoyed no freedoms at all and lived in bondage.
But, in contrast to the people sold to Europeans for export, African serfs and slaves remained recognized members of society….Born into the cultures they served or captured by people similar to them, African slaves were not considered inferior to their masters in anything other than a social sense.  They were accorded basic respect as humans.  (77)

Gates (2002) noted that historically only black slaves of the United States told and wrote about their experiences in bondage.  Gates stated they “… created a genre of literature that at once testified against their captors and bore witness to the urge of every black slave to be free and literate” (1). An excerpt follows from Gates (2002), written by and about, the life of Gustavus Vassa before he was forced into enslavement.

V.  Our land is uncommonly rich and fruitful, and produces all kinds of vegetables in great abundance.  We have plenty of Indian corn, and vast quantities of cotton and tobacco.  Pineapples grow without culture; they are about the size of the largest sugar loaf, and finely flavoured.  We have also spices of different kinds, particularly pepper; and a variety of delicious fruits which I have never seen in Europe; together with gums of various kinds, and honey in abundance.  All our industry is exerted to improve those blessings of nature.  Agriculture is our chief employment; and everyone, even to children and women, is engaged in it.  Thus we are habituated to labour from our earliest years.  Every one contributes something to the common stock: and as we are unacquainted with idleness, we have no beggars.  (pp. 36-37)

Mellon (1988) provides a poignant view of life among enslaved Africans in the United States in the edited text titled Bullwhip Days, a 1930s collection of narratives from former slaves obtained by diverse interviewers from the Federal Writers Project, an adjunct of the U.S. Works Progress Administration.  The following excerpt recalls literacy conditions during the enslavement.

My pappy, he had a stolen ejucation.  ‘At ‘cause his mistress back in South Ca’lina helped him to learn to read an’ write, ‘fo he lef’ there.  You see, in dem days, it was ag’inst de law for slaves to read.  ----James Singleton (197)

Anderson (1988) stated, “Blacks emerged from slavery with a strong belief in the desirability of learning to read and write” (5).  Anderson included an 1879 quote from Harriet Beecher Stowe on the topic of the freedman’s campaign for education.  She stated, “They rushed not to the grog-shop but to the schoolroom—they cried for the spelling-book as bread, and pleaded for teachers as a necessity of life” (5).  John W. Alvord as national superintendent of schools for the Freedmen’s Bureau found what he called “native schools” throughout the entire south.  These schools were founded and maintained exclusively by ex-slaves.  He realized this information was dissonant from existing perceptions about Negro character and that the schools had a self-sustaining quality rooted in the slave experience.  Paramount among these schools were those called “Sabbath” schools or church-operated schools that provided free basic literacy instruction in the evenings and on weekends for thousands not able to attend school during the regular week. Much has taken place since those early days of hopeful literacy development among African Americans. Citations included in the reference list, Franklin and Moss (1994), Ford (1990), and Haberman and Bracey (1997), give further information about literacy conditions since then as well as during the African enslavement.
