Keep Up Parents: Put Reading First
Dr. Jean C. Murphy, professor
Erikson Institute

KEEP UP!  This popular phrase is used by many young people as they attempt to explain new technologies to more senior persons.  The phrase also has currency with parents, family members, and legal guardians (Parents) who are not actively involved in their children’s reading development, and who appear unaware of their critical role in getting children started along the journey of reading. Delpit (1988) suggests, and I concur, that parents must be involved in educational decisions regarding their children.

KEEP UP parents!   An interest in, and appreciation for reading/literacy begins with you, not the schools. Researchers (Dickinson and Tabors, 2001) suggest that reading can begin at home, long before children go to school, from the day they are born.  Parents form the building blocks requisite for formal reading and writing skills taught in school.

Parents can put their children at-risk of reading failure [when they wait five years] for the school system to start the reading process (Hart and Risley, 1995).  Conversely, parents who expose their children, from birth, to an abundance of words, rich vocabularies, and stimulating experiences enable their children to do well in school, and reading in particular.  These children often enter school ready to learn and often perform at high levels.  Children who enter school with weak vocabularies and language skills often fail to catch up, and end up at risk for academic failure.  Torgesen (2004) suggests that children who are poor readers at the end of first grade are almost never able to catch up.  This delay in development affects vocabulary, reading growth, and alters children’s attitudes and motivations to learn.  Parents can influence their child’s progress along the reading continuum by being actively involved from infancy; they can give him/her a head start so that their child is in a state of readiness for learning at the point of school entry.  KEEP UP!

Research (Armbruster, 2001) tells us that children need many and varied opportunities to become skilled readers, and that reading starts in the home as children are read to, sung to, talked with, and listened to.  Simple building-block type activities (e.g., reading, singing, talking, listening, and introducing print and letters) introduced in a fun way can lead to reading enjoyment, and later school success.
Some of the foundational components of reading have their genesis at home with parents as they assume the lead in creating enjoyable experiences with books/literature.  The components include: acquiring vocabulary and language, acquiring phonological awareness, acquiring knowledge of print, acquiring knowledge of letters and words; comprehending meaning; having an awareness of books and other texts; and seeing literacy as a source of enjoyment (Heroman & Jones, 2004).

KEEP UP! This writer suggests that the development of reading skill cannot be the sole responsibility of the school system; parents must step up, and KEEP UP.  They must become informed of and own up to their critical role in the development of reading skills.  Parents should kick start the reading process, readying their children for more formal learning before they enter formal schooling.  The goal is not to hurry reading development, but to aid it. The consequences of parents relying on the school system can be devastating for reading development. It can mean that children can start school at risk of academic failure (Torgesen, 2004). 

The opportunity for parents to influence the reading process is phenomenal in that it doesn’t require formal instruction or training.  Parents don’t have to go outside of the home.  Introducing children to reading and literacy activities can happen right in the crib, on a lap, at the kitchen table.  Parents may facilitate and enhance reading development in a number of ways as suggested below.  However, development should not be hurried or rushed, and parents should be careful to take cues from the behaviors and reactions of their children.

  • Read together through Read-Alouds
  • Talk and listen
  • Learn about the letters of the alphabet through a print-rich home environment
  • Songs, rhymes, and finger plays

Read-Alouds
Research studies and conventional wisdom recommend reading daily to and with your children (infancy through third grade).  “Reading aloud to children has been called the single most important activity for building the knowledge required for success in reading” (Armbruster, 2003).  Mem Fox, author of Reading Magic:  Why Reading Aloud to Our Children Will Change Their Lives Forever, says that “the fire of literacy is created by the emotional sparks between a child, a book, and the person reading.  It isn’t achieved by the book alone, not by the child alone, or by the adult whose reading aloud – it’s the relationship winding between all three, bringing them together in easy harmony.” 
Reading aloud has many benefits, it helps children learn new words, extending their vocabularies; it helps children learn about how the world operates; it helps children learn early reading skills so that they see the connection between spoken and written words.  Reading aloud helps prepare children for school success.

Talking and Listening
Children need many opportunities to be heard, to talk, to use words, to express ideas, and to ask questions about their world and experiences in it.  Parents can provide this opportunity by discussing simple topics of interest to children, and by allowing them to satisfy their natural curiosities through asking questions and participating in discussions about things meaningful to them.

Letter Knowledge through Print-rich Environments
Children exposed to letters of the alphabet through song (ABCs), books, and environmental print have an easier time learning to read.  Parents/family members can provide these experiences by singing and reciting simple songs and nursery rhymes.  Reading books can expose children to letter knowledge.  Pointing out signs in the environment (Stop, School, and Yield) and at the store can help them to gain letter knowledge.  Often the simple activity of pointing out words and letters on children’s clothing can also assist in the development of letter knowledge.

Songs, rhymes and finger plays
Simple songs, rhymes, and finger plays (e.g., Ms. Mary Mack, Mack, Mack, I’m a Little Tea Pot, Five Little Ducks, Open Shut Them) help children hear language, acquire new words, and enjoy the sounds of their native language.   Hearing spoken language with rich cultural intonations is an enriching and enjoyable auditory experience.  These pleasurable experiences set the stage for the enjoyment of visual enrichment through pictures and story books.

Daily experiences between parents and children with any of these activities can help to support reading development and ready children for reading once they enter formal schooling.  Parents are an important link in the journey toward reading success, and ultimately to school success!  KEEP UP parents, KEEP UP!
